WINDI: A Governance Infrastructure for Human–AI Co-Decision Systems

From “Responsible AI” as a property of models to responsibility as an infrastructural condition of interaction

Jober Mögele Correa
WINDI Research Initiative • Kempten, Bavaria, Germany
Chief Governance Officer, WINDI Publishing House
February 2026
Abstract

As artificial intelligence systems increasingly participate in knowledge production and institutional workflows, the question of responsibility can no longer be framed solely at the level of models or regulations. This paper introduces WINDI, an operational governance infrastructure designed to structure real-time interaction between human decision-makers and AI systems. Rather than treating AI as either an autonomous actor or a passive tool, WINDI formalizes a co-decision architecture in which AI processes information, humans retain decision authority, and a governance layer guarantees transparency, traceability, and policy alignment. We argue that this represents a shift from “responsible AI” as a property of systems to responsibility as an infrastructural condition of interaction.

Keywords: AI Governance, Responsibility Infrastructure, Human–AI Co-Decision, EU AI Act, Reflexive Verification, Institutional AI

1. Introduction

Current debates on AI governance focus on three main approaches: regulatory frameworks defining compliance requirements; ethical principles published by technology companies; and post-hoc audit tools assessing AI outputs.

While these approaches are necessary, they share a common limitation: governance is positioned outside the moment of human–AI interaction.

This paper presents an alternative: governance as an operational layer embedded within the interaction itself.

2. From Tools to Co-Decision Systems

Traditional computing assumed a simple hierarchy: human decides, machine executes. Contemporary AI systems alter this dynamic fundamentally: AI processes, human interprets, and responsibility becomes diffuse.

WINDI addresses this shift by formalizing the interaction as a co-decision system with clearly defined roles:

Actor Role
AI Processing, analysis, suggestion
Human Final decision authority, responsibility
Governance Infrastructure (WINDI) Ensures transparency, context-awareness, and policy alignment

This structure does not anthropomorphize AI, nor does it reduce humans to supervisors. Instead, it institutionalizes the interaction.

3. Responsibility as Infrastructure

Most “responsible AI” initiatives treat responsibility as a feature of models. WINDI redefines responsibility as an infrastructure surrounding decisions.

This infrastructure provides:

  • Context detection — institutional, legal, and situational awareness
  • Policy application — risk level assessment and communication norms
  • Explanation — human-readable reasoning for governance decisions
  • Interface manifestation — visual representation of governance state
  • Traceability — receipts, cryptographic hashes, and authorship context

Responsibility thus becomes observable and inspectable, not merely declared.

4. The Six-Phase Operational Cycle

WINDI formalizes AI–human interaction as a six-phase cycle:

  1. 1
    Perception — Detection of contextual and institutional signals
  2. 2
    Cognition — Interpretation of governance implications
  3. 3
    Governance — Application of policy rules
  4. 4
    Explanation — Communication of reasoning to the human
  5. 5
    Interface — Visual manifestation of governance state
  6. 6
    Science — Documentation and formalization of the process

This final phase introduces reflexive verification: the system’s own documentation can be produced under the same governance conditions it describes.

5. Reflexive Verification and Epistemic Accountability

A defining feature of WINDI is that the infrastructure governing AI-assisted work can also govern the documentation describing itself. This creates a rare form of epistemic accountability, where:

Core Proposition

The explanation of a governance system is itself produced under that system’s governance.

This moves AI governance from declarative claims to inspectable production trails.

Evidence of this property has been demonstrated in production: press materials describing the WINDI system were authored within the WINDI-governed A4 Desk BABEL editor, carrying verifiable governance metadata including cryptographic receipts, authorship context, and institutional witness signatures.

6. Implications for Policy and Institutions

WINDI suggests a paradigm shift in how AI governance is conceptualized:

Current Framing → Proposed Shift
Responsible AI models → Responsibility infrastructures
Post-hoc audits → In-process governance
Compliance checklists → Operational accountability layers

This model aligns with emerging regulatory demands, particularly the EU AI Act, while preserving the principle of human sovereignty in decision-making. It offers institutions a concrete architectural response to the challenge of AI accountability: governance is not bolted on after deployment, but woven into the fabric of interaction.

7. Conclusion

WINDI demonstrates that AI governance can be embedded directly into the interaction between humans and AI systems. By treating responsibility as infrastructure rather than attribute, it establishes a new paradigm for accountable hybrid decision environments.

AI processes. Humans decide. Infrastructure guarantees governance.

The system presented here is not a theoretical proposal. It is an operational infrastructure, documented across multiple technical reports, deployed on production servers, and demonstrably capable of governing its own public disclosure. We invite the research community, policymakers, and institutional practitioners to examine the evidence, engage with the model, and contribute to the development of governance infrastructures adequate to the hybrid environments we now inhabit.
